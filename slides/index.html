The Problem

Students at Emily Carr struggle to navigate the library independently. The Library of Congress classification system is unfamiliar, physical signage is minimal, and most students don't know what services and resources the library offers beyond printing. Staff are stretched thin handling basic wayfinding questions instead of providing research support.

Our Solution

A Raspberry Pi touchscreen kiosk at the library entrance — providing self-service wayfinding, catalogue search, account management, and a community bulletin board. It bridges the gap between digital information and physical space, empowering students to explore everything the library offers without always needing staff.

More than wayfinding — a community hub.

Designed for first-year students, international students, community borrowers, and library staff alike.

Friction & Mystery

Friction Points

"The bathroom and elevator are hidden knowledge"
  • No wayfinding signage beyond the main floor
  • Library of Congress system completely unfamiliar — students don't know where to start
  • Stacks map is a single sheet of paper, hard to read
  • Signage on pillars has low contrast — pillar colour and text colour are too similar, making wayfinding nearly invisible
  • Expired posters and outdated event info on bulletin boards
  • Services promoted only through posters and Instagram — neither guarantees student reach
  • Printing issues consume most staff time at the desk

Mysteries

"The locked room on the lower level"
  • Rare books room always locked — students don't know it exists or how to access it
  • Fibre library, mannequins, and artist books hidden in lower level
  • Study rooms, hammock, and board games — most students never discover them
  • Community borrower access — public visitors unsure if they're welcome
  • Tea station and bike pump — "hidden perks" no one talks about

Design Precedents

Indigo / Chapters

Pioneered in-store kiosks for book discovery. Early units looked like staff workstations — discouraged use. Moved to dedicated touchscreen hardware. Keyboard removed.

Takeaway: Hardware form matters as much as UI.

VPL / Burnaby PL

RFID self-checkout. Multi-language support. Account management (loans, holds, fines). Multiple kiosks at entrance/exit areas.

Takeaway: Build features relevant to the user's actual tasks.

Airport Wayfinding

Interactive touchscreen directories. Floor maps with "you are here." Multi-language. Designed for stressed, unfamiliar users navigating complex spaces.

Takeaway: Design for anxiety — simplicity reduces cognitive load.

Campus Digital Signage

Event boards at university entrances. Rotating community content — clubs, workshops, lost & found. Students engage with what's timely and local.

Takeaway: Community content drives repeat engagement.

McDonald's Order Kiosks

Replaced counter queues with self-service touchscreens. NFC tap-to-pay. Reduced staff workload for routine tasks. Users prefer autonomy.

Takeaway: Self-service frees staff for higher-value interactions.

Jakob's Law: "Users prefer systems they already understand. Mental models matter."

User Testing Plan

Date

April 7, 2026

Format

3 rounds × 20 minutes each

Participants

Cross-section of library users

Three Task Scenarios:
  1. Search & Locate: Find a specific book and navigate to shelf
  2. Ask for Help: Connect with librarian for research support
  3. Explore & Discover: Browse events and services
Method: Think-aloud protocol + post-task questions + team debrief between rounds

Evaluation Criteria

Task Success

  • Can users complete core tasks without guidance?
  • Time to completion — baseline vs. benchmark
  • Error recovery — can they self-correct?

Discovery & Learning

  • Is the wayfinding map helpful?
  • Do users discover services they didn't know about?
  • What causes hesitation or confusion?

Project Reflection

What We Learned

  • Service design is about orchestrating touchpoints across time and space — not building a single artifact
  • Research with real users and staff revealed needs we never would have anticipated from assumptions alone
  • The library is far more than a book collection — it's a community hub, emotional support space, and creative resource

How We Grew

  • Iterating from paper sketches to hi-fi prototype taught us the value of testing early and often
  • Collaborating across four disciplines (SR, XD, TM, VC) showed how design thinking bridges different expertise
  • Designing for accessibility and inclusion isn't an afterthought — it's foundational to good service design
"We came in wanting to build a kiosk. We left understanding that we were designing a relationship between people and place."
