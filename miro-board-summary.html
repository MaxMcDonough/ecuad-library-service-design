The Library Kiosk: Find Your Voice, Find Your Community

An interactive Raspberry Pi station that helps students navigate the library, manage their student life, and find their people — without hiding behind a screen.
We're building an interactive kiosk powered by a Raspberry Pi that lives in the ECUAD library. Think of it as a mix between an Indigo Chapters wayfinding station, a student services portal, and a community bulletin board — all in one physical touchpoint.
It handles the practical stuff: find where a book lives in the stacks, check out materials, top up your print card, pay tuition balances, manage your student account. But that's just the surface.
The real purpose is community. The kiosk has a living bulletin board where students can post activities — "I'm going hiking this Saturday, who's in?" — and other students can join. It syncs to personal calendars and sends Slack or email blasts to people who've flagged those interests. Game nights, movie screenings, study groups, art shows, talks — all surfaced in one place, created by students for students.
The core design principle: This kiosk does NOT replace human interaction or let students retreat into their shells. It's a confidence builder. It gives students — especially those who are shy, new, international, or unsure — the information and the invitation they need to engage with the library, the staff, and each other. Find your voice. Find your community.

Feature Modules

📚

Wayfinding & StacksIndigo Chapters-style

Search for materials and see exactly where they are — floor, section, shelf. Visual map of the library with real-time availability.

  • Search by title, author, course, or topic
  • Floor-by-floor interactive map
  • Highlights hidden collections (film, archives, special collections)
  • Shows what's available to borrow (games, equipment, plinths)
💳

Student Account HubFees, cards, balances

Handle the admin stuff without waiting in line. One place for everything financial and account-related.

  • Top up print card balance
  • Pay tuition fees or account balances
  • Check out and return books
  • View account status and dues
  • Master your student account from one screen
📌

Community BoardBulletin board + event creation

A living, student-powered bulletin board. Browse what's happening, or post your own thing and watch people join.

  • Browse upcoming events, game nights, talks, screenings
  • Post your own: "Hiking Saturday — who's in?"
  • Other students tap to join
  • Filter by interest: outdoors, games, art, social, academic
  • Staff can also post library events and workshops
🔔

Smart NotificationsCalendar + Slack + email

When you join an event, it doesn't just disappear into a list. It becomes real — synced and shared.

  • Auto-adds events to your personal calendar
  • Sends Slack or email blast to interested students
  • Notifications based on flagged interests
  • "3 people from your program are going to Board Game Night"
  • Reminders before events start
🤝

Confidence & ConnectionThe soul of the kiosk

Every screen, every interaction is designed to nudge students toward people — not away from them.

  • Prompts: "Have a question? Sarah at the desk loves this topic"
  • "5 students near you are also looking for study partners"
  • Warm, inclusive language throughout
  • Multilingual support for international students
  • Designed for the person who's most unsure — if it works for them, it works for everyone
🌍

Upcoming Events FeedLibrary + student-created

Everything that's happening, in one feed. No more hidden flyers or word-of-mouth-only events.

  • Library workshops, writing centre sessions
  • Student-organized game nights, movie nights
  • Club events, talks, screenings
  • Resource swap drops and art supply exchanges
  • Sign up on the spot — one tap

Design Principles

Gateway, Not a Wall

The kiosk starts interactions, it doesn't end them. Every feature bridges students to staff, events, or each other.

Voice Before Efficiency

We're not optimizing for speed. We're building confidence. A student who feels welcome will come back. A fast transaction won't.

Designed for the Unsure

Multicultural students, foundation-year, introverts, first-gen — if the most hesitant person feels welcome, everyone does.

Community Is the Product

The kiosk is a tool. The community is the outcome. Every feature should make someone feel less alone.

Research Findings That Led Us Here

Main Floor = Print Shop

Most front-desk activity is printing, card loading, and troubleshooting. The library entrance reads as a transactional service counter, not a place to explore or linger.

Lower Level = The Real Library

Plants, natural light, beanbags, hammock, compact shelving with archival film materials. People come down here to stay. Completely different energy from upstairs.

Wayfinding Is Broken

Bathrooms are hidden. Elevator use is ambiguous. Book borrowing process has no clear signage. Return sign is blocked by a display case. Beginners are lost.

Hidden Services Everywhere

Games lending, resource swap, bookable rooms, special collections — they exist but almost nobody knows about them. No visible communication strategy.

Staff Are Great but Anchored

Friendly, approachable, always helpful — but confined to the front desk. Zero staff presence on lower or middle floors. Interactions are almost entirely operational.

Public Space, Blurred Boundaries

The whole main floor is public. Non-ECU visitors use computers and the library without signing in. No clear boundary between library and general building.

Physical Access Is a Barrier

No first-floor entrance — students must go upstairs to enter. Security alarm no longer works, so the restriction is pointless. Dead plants waste desk space. Second-floor wing feels unstable and underused. (Jenny)

Project Direction — The Kiosk Concept
New
The team has converged on a direction: an interactive Raspberry Pi kiosk that combines Indigo-style wayfinding, student account management, a community bulletin board with event creation, and smart notifications (calendar sync + Slack/email). Core principle: this is a confidence builder and community connector — not a self-service machine that replaces people.
Team
Core Concept
Interactive RPi kiosk. Wayfinding + student account + community board + notifications. Physical hardware in the library. Touchscreen interface. Accessible, inclusive, community-first.
Max
Wayfinding — Indigo Chapters Style
Search the stacks like you'd search in a bookstore kiosk. Floor-by-floor map. See exactly where materials live. Highlight the hidden stuff — film archives, games, special collections, the resource swap.
Max
Student Account Hub
Top up print cards. Pay tuition fees or balances. Check out and return books. Master your student account from one screen. All the stuff that currently clogs the front desk.
Max
Community Board — The Big Idea
Student-powered bulletin board. "I want to go hiking Saturday" — post it, others join, it adds to their calendar and sends a Slack/email to interested people. Board games, movie nights, talks, study groups. Make the invisible community visible.
Max
Smart Notifications
When you join something, it syncs to your calendar and sends blasts to people with matching interests. "3 people from your program are going to Board Game Night." Reminders before events. Interest-based filtering.
Team
Design Principle — Gateway, Not a Wall
The kiosk doesn't replace staff or student interaction. It gives students information and confidence so they CAN engage. It bridges to people, not substitutes for them.
Team
Design Principle — Voice Before Efficiency
We're not optimizing for speed. We're building confidence. A student who feels welcome will come back. Designed for multicultural students, foundation-year, introverts, anyone who feels unsure.
Team
What This Is NOT
Not a self-checkout machine. Not a replacement for staff. Not something that lets students hide. It's a tool that builds confidence, surfaces community, and opens doors to connection.

Jenny flagged that the library's physical layout creates barriers — no first-floor entrance, dead-end wings, wasted space. The kiosk should also address physical navigation, not just digital wayfinding. If students can't easily get into the building, a screen inside won't help.
