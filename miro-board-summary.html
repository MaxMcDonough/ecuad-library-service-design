Project Direction — Locked In

The Library Kiosk: Find Your Voice, Find Your Community

An interactive Raspberry Pi station that helps students navigate the library — while building confidence, inclusion, and connection.
We're building an interactive kiosk powered by a Raspberry Pi that lives in the library and helps students find materials in the stacks, discover upcoming events, sign up for game nights and activities, check out books, and top up their print cards. But here's what makes this different from a self-service machine: the goal isn't to replace human interaction — it's to encourage it.
The kiosk is designed as a gateway, not a wall. It gives students — especially those who are shy, new, international, or unsure how to ask for help — the information and confidence they need to engage with the library, the staff, and each other. It's about accessibility and inclusion first. We want students to find their voice, not retreat into a screen.

What the Kiosk Does

🔍

Find Resources

Search the stacks, locate materials by floor and section, see what's available. No more wandering or guessing.

📅

Discover & Sign Up

Browse upcoming events, game nights, workshops. Sign up on the spot. Make the library's hidden services visible.

📦

Check Out & Print

Borrow books, update print cards — the practical stuff that currently clogs the front desk and eats staff time.

🤝

Bridge to People

The kiosk doesn't end the interaction — it starts it. Prompts to talk to staff, join groups, connect with other students. Confidence first.

🌍

Accessible & Inclusive

Designed for multicultural students, foundation-year students, and anyone who feels unsure. Low barrier, high welcome.

🏠

Community Hub

Not a replacement for people — a tool that helps students find their community. Safety, belonging, and voice.

Design Principles

Gateway, Not a Wall

The kiosk starts interactions, it doesn't end them. Every feature bridges students to staff, events, or each other.

Voice Before Efficiency

We're not optimizing for speed. We're building confidence. A student who feels welcome will come back.

Designed for the Unsure

Multicultural students, foundation-year, introverts, first-gen — if the most hesitant person feels welcome, everyone does.

Community Is the Product

The kiosk is a tool. The community is the outcome. Every feature should make someone feel less alone.

Research Findings That Led Us Here

Main Floor = Print Shop

Most front-desk activity is printing, card loading, and troubleshooting. The library entrance reads as a transactional service counter, not a place to explore or linger.

Lower Level = The Real Library

Plants, natural light, beanbags, hammock, compact shelving with archival film materials. People come down here to stay. Completely different energy from upstairs.

Wayfinding Is Broken

Bathrooms are hidden. Elevator use is ambiguous. Book borrowing process has no clear signage. Return sign is blocked by a display case. Beginners are lost.

Hidden Services Everywhere

Games lending, resource swap, bookable rooms, special collections — they exist but almost nobody knows about them. No visible communication strategy.

Staff Are Great but Anchored

Friendly, approachable, always helpful — but confined to the front desk. Zero staff presence on lower or middle floors. Interactions are almost entirely operational.

Public Space, Blurred Boundaries

The whole main floor is public. Non-ECU visitors use computers and the library without signing in. No clear boundary between library and general building.

Physical Access Is a Barrier

No first-floor entrance — students must go upstairs to enter. Security alarm no longer works, so the restriction is pointless. Dead plants waste desk space. Second-floor wing feels unstable and underused. (Jennie)

Do senior students feel more comfortable asking library staff for help than foundation students? What affects comfort levels and service accessibility?
Jennie
No First-Floor Entrance
There is no entrance on the first floor, which is inconvenient. Students are required to go up to the second floor just to enter the library. Staff said this was for security reasons, but the alarm system is no longer working — people can walk out without any checks. A first-floor entrance would make the library much more accessible.
Jennie
Dead Plants & Wasted Space
There are too many plants in the area, and many of them are dead. This space could be better used for student desks and study areas instead of unnecessary decoration.
Jennie
Second-Floor Wing Feels Unstable
The second-floor wing feels unstable and is rarely used. It could be redesigned into a study space by adding desks near the glass windows, creating a more open, fresh, and inviting environment.
Lyon
Research Desk — Intimidating
People find the research desk intimidating. Students ask questions for personal uses — reputable sources for news, even food recommendations. The desk could be more approachable.
Lyon
Awareness & Signage Gaps
Signage would help people understand what the computers are used for. Signage that lets people know they're allowed to eat. Signage for mobility needs. Signage for washrooms. Basic wayfinding is missing.
Lyon
No Entrance on First Floor
Prior security concerns around people stealing things. There's no longer that concern — removed security sensors, therefore no reason not to have a first-floor entrance. Would allow for great accessibility.

Section 4
Service Direction — Interactive Library Kiosk
Active
Team-agreed direction. An interactive RPi kiosk: wayfinding + student account + community board + notifications. Physical hardware in the library with a touchscreen interface. Accessible, inclusive, community-first.
Team
The Direction
Interactive RPi kiosk. Wayfinding + student account + community board + notifications. Physical hardware in the library. Touchscreen interface. Accessible, inclusive, community-first.
Max
Wayfinding — Indigo Chapters Style
Search the stacks like you'd search in a bookstore kiosk. Floor-by-floor map. See exactly where materials live. Highlight the hidden stuff — film archives, games, special collections, the resource swap.
Max
Student Account Hub
Top up print cards. Pay tuition fees or balances. Check out and return books. Master your student account from one screen. All the stuff that currently clogs the front desk.
Max
Community Board — The Big Idea
Student-powered bulletin board. "I want to go hiking Saturday" — post it, others join, it adds to their calendar and sends a Slack/email to interested people. Board games, movie nights, talks, study groups. Make the invisible community visible.
Max
Smart Notifications
When you join something, it syncs to your calendar and sends blasts to people with matching interests. "3 people from your program are going to Board Game Night." Reminders before events. Interest-based filtering.
Team
Design Principle — Gateway, Not a Wall
The kiosk doesn't replace staff or student interaction. It gives students information and confidence so they CAN engage. It bridges to people, not substitutes for them.
Team
Design Principle — Voice Before Efficiency
We're not optimizing for speed. We're building confidence. A student who feels welcome will come back. Designed for multicultural students, foundation-year, introverts, anyone who feels unsure.
Team
What This Is NOT
Not a self-checkout machine. Not a replacement for staff. Not something that lets students hide. It's a tool that builds confidence, surfaces community, and opens doors to connection.
Jennie
Accessibility Is Physical Too
Jennie flagged that the library's physical layout creates barriers — no first-floor entrance, dead-end wings, wasted space. The kiosk should also address physical navigation, not just digital wayfinding. If students can't easily get into the building, a screen inside won't help.
Lyon
Lyon's Direction — Signage First
Lyon's research consistently points to signage as the number one gap. The kiosk should function as a digital signage solution — wayfinding, awareness of what's available (eating allowed, washrooms, mobility access, computer usage). This reinforces the "gateway" principle.
Alicia
Alicia's Direction — Precedents
Three key precedents: (1) Indigo/Chapters catalogue search — bookstore kiosk model for browsing and finding items, (2) VPL/BPL check-out desk — public library self-service model, (3) Community-style bulletin board — student-driven event posting. Also noted that VPL, EPL, and TPL online catalogues all share nearly identical design layouts regardless of province.
Precedents
Design Precedents & Reference Points
Research
Design precedents that inform our kiosk direction. These are existing systems and experiences that solve similar problems — catalogue search, physical check-out, community boards, and public library catalogue UX patterns.
Alicia
Indigo/Chapters Catalogue Search
In-store kiosks at Indigo/Chapters let customers search the full catalogue, check availability, and locate items by aisle. This is the closest precedent to our wayfinding module — a self-serve search station that bridges digital catalogue to physical space.
Alicia
VPL/BPL Check-Out Desk
Vancouver and Burnaby public libraries use self-checkout kiosks alongside staffed desks. The kiosk handles the transactional stuff (borrow, return, card management) so staff can focus on research help and human interaction. Exactly the "gateway not a wall" principle.
Alicia
Community-Style Bulletin Board
Physical bulletin boards in community centres and co-ops where people post events, meetups, and offers. Our community board module is the digital version — student-powered, interest-matched, calendar-synced. The key insight is that the best community tools feel informal and low-pressure.
Alicia
VPL / EPL / TPL Online Catalogues
Vancouver, Edmonton, and Toronto public library online catalogues all share a nearly identical design layout — no matter which province you're in. This standardized UX pattern (search bar, filters by format, availability indicators, place-hold buttons) is what students already know from public libraries. Our kiosk search should feel familiar, not foreign.

These are the questions we want to bring to the library staff on Friday. A mix of our original research questions plus new ones informed by the kiosk direction and Jennie's observations about physical access. Each team member should come prepared with follow-ups in their area.
Team — Original
Q1 — How do students discover services?
Games lending, resource swap, bookable rooms, special collections — how do students find out these exist? Is there a strategy or just word of mouth?
Team — Original
Q2 — What's the deal with the locked room?
What's the process for accessing the special collections room? How often do students request access? Why is it closed by default?
Team — Original
Q3 — Public vs. student access
The main floor is open to non-ECU visitors. How does the library balance student needs with public use, especially around shared resources like computers?
Team — Original
Q4 — What do people actually ask at the desk?
Most interactions seem to be printing/card-loading. Is that accurate? Are there conversations staff wish happened more — like research help or material recommendations?
Team — Original
Q5 — Wayfinding & signage plans?
Current signage focuses on events and room booking. Any plans for better spatial orientation — bathrooms, elevator, floor layout — especially for new or international students?
Jennie — New
Q6 — Why no first-floor entrance?
Why is there no entrance on the first floor? Are there any plans to add one? The alarm system no longer works, so the original security reason seems outdated.
Jennie — New
Q7 — Second-floor wing potential?
What is the second-floor wing currently used for? Could it be redesigned as a student study area with desks near the glass windows?
Jennie — New
Q8 — Dead plants / space usage
Are there plans to improve the social area — replacing dead plants with student desks and study areas? Is student feedback considered when making these changes?
Max — New (Kiosk)
Q9 — Would a kiosk help or hurt?
If there was an interactive station in the library that helped students find resources, discover events, and sign up for activities — would staff see that as helpful or as something that pulls students away from the desk?
Max — New (Kiosk)
Q10 — What do staff wish students knew?
If you could magically make every student aware of one thing about the library, what would it be? What's the most underused service or space?
Max — New (Community)
Q11 — Events & community building
How does the library currently promote events like game nights and workshops? Would there be interest in students being able to create and post their own community events through the library?
